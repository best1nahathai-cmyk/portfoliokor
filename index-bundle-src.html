UX Research · Case study

UX RESEARCH & DESIGN

Redesigning how Bangkok commuters view, navigate, and manage their transit transaction history — a feature that did not exist in the app at all before this project.

RoleProduct Designer Year2026 Scope06 chapters StatusDocumented
The starting point

Visibility and trust — clear from the start.

The brief established that users needed a way to view their transit transaction history directly inside the PlayCard app. Before this project, that capability simply did not exist. If an MRT commuter wanted to see what they had spent or where they had travelled, their only option was to walk up to a physical kiosk machine at the station and print a receipt.

The requirement board included presentation slides from the business team, a photograph of an actual physical kiosk receipt as a reference for what data fields needed to be displayed, and an initial framing of the problem from the product side. The core user need was clear from the start: visibility and trust.

What we needed to build
  • View transit history in-app
  • Display correct data fields
  • Support dispute & refund flows

Need · 01
View transit history in-app

Eliminate the need to visit a kiosk to see basic spend and travel history. Bring it into the app, on the device commuters already carry.

Need · 02
Display correct data fields

Mirror what the physical receipt shows — station, time, fare — so the in-app version reads as trustworthy and complete.

Need · 03
Support dispute & refund flows

A pathway from "something looks wrong" to resolution, without forcing users back out into the physical station to complain.

Honest audit

Map what already exists before designing what should.

Before any redesign work began, I mapped out what was already live in the app. Understanding the existing system is not optional. It is the only honest way to know what is actually broken versus what just feels uncomfortable.

The existing Transaction History flow required users to pass through the Card Detail screen before reaching their history. This created an unnecessary detour. The filter flow was circular and disorienting. Checking balance was a completely separate path with no connection to history. And when something went wrong, there was no clear path to resolution from within the app.

Issue · 01 Unnecessary detour via Card Detail Users wanting history had to navigate through card management first — a wrong mental model.
Issue · 02 Circular filter flow Applying a filter looped users back into the same screens repeatedly. No sense of progress.
Issue · 03 Balance and history disconnected Check Balance was a separate path with no logical connection to the transaction history.
Issue · 04 No error recourse in-app When something went wrong, the app gave no in-path forward. Resolution required a station visit.
Two answers, one discussion

Bring options, not conclusions.

Rather than designing one solution and presenting it as the answer, I developed two distinct design directions to bring to the stakeholder discussion.

Chosen by PO Direction · 01
History separated by card

A separate history feed per card. Treats each card as its own financial surface and stakeholder hierarchy. Encourages low overview switching.

Direction · 02
All activity in one place

A unified feed combining transit, top-ups and statement-style charges. Greater financial overview, fewer screens, higher discoverability.

Decision: the chosen direction was the one keeping a clear and clean view, with the Product Owner. The decision was rooted in the platform's existing design language — and the principles already used elsewhere in the app.

Both paths, both states

The work split into two parallel flows.

After aligning with the Product Owner on direction, I took the chosen approach and expanded it into a complete flow covering not just the happy path but every branch, error case, and edge state the system would need to handle.

Confirmed Card Flow · Normal Path
Transit History — Normal Path
  1. Play Card Menu
  2. Card Detail
  3. Transit Summary
  4. Transit History (Full List)
  5. Transit Details

Key change: Transit Summary acts as an intermediate layer giving an at-a-glance balance before users tap into the full list — without forcing a Card Detail load.

Confirmed Error Flow · Edge States
When Something Goes Wrong
  1. Transit History — Registration Issue
  2. Pending Status — Alert
  3. Input Stop Verification
  4. Confirmation
  5. OTP Notification
  6. Updated Transit Details

Every error case routes the user toward a clear, in-app resolution path. No more "go visit a station" dead ends.

Mental-model alignment

Ground the design in what users already recognise.

Before finalising the wireframes, I conducted competitive research to validate the design patterns I was building and understand what users were already familiar with.

The research focused on real transit and payment apps, especially stronger market examples already operating in similar spaces, including the primary MRT ecosystem. Since PlayCard existed as a secondary experience within a larger app environment, it was important to study how leading platforms structured these flows in practice.

The goal was not to copy competitors. It was to understand the mental models users had already built from the apps they used most often, so PlayCard could align with familiar behaviours and reduce unnecessary learning. By grounding the design in patterns users already recognised, the experience could feel more intuitive from the first tap.

Benchmark · 01
BMV Apps (Market)

BVA's transit history flow with standard list view, filter panel, fares results, individual transaction detail, and clear flow action — all within Bangkok's most familiar MRT app.

Benchmark · 02
PaaMaegmum App (revisit)

Booming app design from a brand based in a market we've adopted: balancing financial overview screen mid-route navigation, dispute initiation entry points and edit access.

Benchmark · 03
Filter UX Patterns

How leading apps structure filters: bottom sheets, sticky chips, and where users tend to tap most readily without falling into circular loops.

What the research produced
  • Transit components and connected steps, success state after engagement, and matching transactions to selected filters — aligned to be expressive for transit-grade research in this domain.
  • Transactional / transaction structures — common formats already proven across selected expressive flows, so users feel oriented from the first tap.
  • Empty state messaging — clear, reassuring language without leaving the user wondering whether something is actually broken.
